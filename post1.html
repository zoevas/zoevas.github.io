An health ontology for disaster management

I designed a health ontology to assist in triaging victims during disasters. This domain-specific ontology extends the Basic Formal Ontology (BFO), providing a structured framework for representing medical knowledge in emergency scenarios. The Basic Formal Ontology (BFO) is a small, domain-neutral top-level ontology designed to organize and integrate biomedical and other types of information. It provides a modular framework used by many biomedical ontologies, such as the Gene Ontology and Foundational Model of Anatomy. BFO divides all entities into two main categories: Continuants, which persist through time, and Occurrents, which unfold or happen over time. This distinction helps structure complex domain-specific knowledge consistently and systematically.

For each victim, the ontology models key information including their medical health record, vital signs, and triage status, enabling more efficient and consistent decision-making in critical situations.

In this ontology, the relationships between diseases, medications, and their corresponding effects are automatically inferred through OWL restrictions. Specifically, each disease class is linked to its physiological or clinical consequences using a diseaseHasEffect object property, while each medication class is associated with the effects it produces via medicationHasEffect. By defining OWL class restrictions—for example, specifying that a particular disease must have a certain effect or that a medication must produce a certain effect—these relationships are implicitly enforced in the ontology. As a result, any instance of a disease or medication automatically inherits the corresponding effect relationships, reducing manual annotation and ensuring consistency across the knowledge base.

As an example, in the ontology, a disease class can have restrictions linking it to specific physiological effects. For instance, the disease class Transplants (ICD10_Z94) is modeled with the following OWL restriction: 

<owl:Class rdf:about="http://www.semanticweb.org/certh/nightingale#ICD10_Z94">
    <rdfs:subClassOf rdf:resource="http://www.semanticweb.org/certh/nightingale#Disease"/>
    <rdfs:subClassOf>
        <owl:Restriction>
            <owl:onProperty rdf:resource="http://www.semanticweb.org/certh/nightingale#diseaseHasEffect"/>
            <owl:hasValue rdf:resource="http://www.semanticweb.org/certh/nightingale#limit_response_to_hypotension"/>
        </owl:Restriction>
    </rdfs:subClassOf>
    <nightingale:icd10code>ICD10_Z94</nightingale:icd10code>
    <nightingale:snomedCUI>C0332835</nightingale:snomedCUI>
    <nightingale:snomedID rdf:resource="http://purl.bioontology.org/ontology/SNOMEDCT/24486003"/>
    <rdfs:label>Transplants</rdfs:label>
</owl:Class>

This restriction specifies that the disease class Transplants has a physiological effect, limit_response_to_hypotension, through the diseaseHasEffect property. By including ICD-10 codes (like ICD10_Z94) and SNOMED IDs (like C0332835), the ontology supports federated queries across biomedical databases. This allows retrieving additional information from external resources, improving interoperability and enriching decision support during emergency scenarios.
